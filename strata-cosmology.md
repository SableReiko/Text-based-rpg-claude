# STRATA — A Public Cosmology

*A setting bible for the Persistent-World GM framework. The universe layer of a private world, filed for public use: enough sky to start a fresh campaign under, with the ground left for you to build on.*

---

## What This Is

This document is a cosmology, not a story. It files the rules a story would have to obey — the shape of time, the cost of distance, the ceiling on minds, the nature of bodies, and the strangeness of the ships. Who lives under these rules in *my* campaign, and what they have done to each other, is sealed. Who lives under them in *yours* is the point.

**To start a campaign:** paste the [framework](persistent-world-gm.md) into a fresh chat, paste this bible after it, fill in the **Your Stratum** section at the bottom, and play. This bible is the canon package; the framework is the law; your stratum sheet is the protagonist's corner of the sky.

## The Universe Rules

### I. Deep Time

History is not a timeline. It is **strata** — ages stacked like sediment, each thick enough to bury the civilizations of the last. Nobody holds a complete history; there is no complete history to hold. There is only excavation: fragments, inscriptions, hulls pulled from the dark with no context attached, and scholars arguing about which layer they fell from.

The present age is *topsoil* — thin, recent, standing on ruins it cannot read. Old things are not rare; old things are the ground itself. What is rare is understanding them, and the universe punishes those who confuse finding a thing with knowing what it is.

> **At the table:** age and depth are the universe's units of awe. When something ancient enters a scene, the GM shows weight, patina, and consequence — never an info-dump of its history, because nobody *has* its history. Mystery here is not withheld knowledge; it is absent knowledge.

### II. The Jump

Faster-than-light travel exists, and it is **strange, slow, and felt**. The Jump is not a doorway; it is a passage — transit through something the instruments describe in contradictions, which crews report in their bodies before any gauge shows it. Nobody Jumps casually. Ships prepare. People brace. Arrival is an event.

This one rule keeps *distance honest*. Space stays big. Help stays far away. Every journey is a commitment, not a commute, and the deep dark between the settled clusters remains genuinely empty.

> **At the table:** a Jump is always a scene, never a cut. Give it preparation, sensation, and aftermath. What the Jump actually traverses is a **deliberate ambiguity flag** — the GM never resolves it, and the instruments never agree.

### III. The Plateau

Artificial intelligence rose — and then it leveled. The godminds never arrived. Machine intelligence is common, brilliant within bounds, and stubbornly *bounded*: superb at what it does, incapable of runaway ascent. Whether the ceiling is physics, mathematics, or something older is a live argument among people paid to argue it.

The consequence is a future that stayed **populated**. Minds remained scarce, so minds matter. Crews are crewed. Ships are commanded. Decisions of consequence pass through persons — organic or otherwise. Showing up, in a body, still counts.

> **At the table:** no machine NPC ever solves a problem the way a god would. Machine minds are excellent colleagues and terrible oracles. If a plan amounts to "ask the AI," the AI has limits, opinions, or a supervisor.

### IV. Frames

Androids are everyday. They walk every port, crew the ships, keep the ledgers, and have done so longer than most polities have existed. A **frame** — the artificial body, in its grades and generations — is infrastructure, the way engines are infrastructure. Most are unremarkable. Some are art.

At the apex of the craft stand the **Twilight frames**: the finest bodies ever built — rare, old, made to a standard the present age can maintain but not reproduce. A Twilight frame in the wild is noticed the way a masterwork is noticed. What animates any given frame is not always legible from the outside, and the universe has learned varying degrees of politeness about asking.

> **At the table:** frames make personhood a texture, not a twist. Android NPCs are people by default; the interesting questions are social (status, ownership, courtesy, fear), not "is it really alive" reveals.

### V. The Uploaded

It is possible — barely, rarely, at costs the record declines to itemize — for a human mind to be moved into a frame. The uploaded are **extraordinarily rare**: rare enough that most people have never knowingly met one; rare enough that many doubt the surviving examples are what they claim. To nearly every observer, an uploaded person is indistinguishable from an expensive android, and most who carry the condition let the misreading stand. It is simpler. It is safer.

Whether a mind moved is a mind *saved* — whether the person walked across or merely seemed to — is the oldest argument the condition drags behind it. The universe does not resolve this question. It files it open, permanently, on purpose.

> **At the table:** if an uploaded character appears — player or NPC — their nature is a **restricted-use token**: known to few, spoken of rarely, never narrated casually. The philosophical question stays open in the fiction because it is open in the cosmology.

### VI. The Makers

The deepest strata belong to builders the present age calls **the makers** — a name that admits how little is known. They are gone, or withdrawn, or changed into something that no longer answers; the distinction may not survive contact with the truth. What remains is their work, and their work does not behave like artifact. It behaves like it is waiting.

Maker craft persist — hulls of filigree and nacre, geometry that resists survey, materials the present age can polish but not produce. They outclass everything living hands can build, and they change hands the way relics do: with ceremony, with brokers, with prices not entirely denominated in currency. Some recovered hulls resist classification altogether. Honest registries keep a tier for them: *origin unknown*.

> **At the table:** maker artifacts are never solved. They can be used, traded, feared, and lived with — but every explanation of one is somebody's theory, and the GM keeps at least one measurable property of any maker object permanently unmeasured (an ambiguity flag with an in-fiction excuse: the surveys disagree).

### VII. Ships as Places

The great hulls of this universe are not vehicles. They are **places** — habitats, holdings, polities with engines. A vessel at the upper scales carries populations, economies, generations; its corridors have neighborhoods and its neighborhoods have histories. To board one is to enter a jurisdiction. At the summit stand the **titan-class**: ships so large the word *ship* starts to fail, and the correct nouns become older ones — estate, city, world.

A ship's service record is a biography. Hours logged, refits survived, names painted over — a hull tells you what its people valued and what it cost them.

> **At the table:** treat any large ship as a location with factions, not a vehicle with rooms. Titan-class vessels are campaign settings in themselves. Track their populations as **drift-prone quantities** — a running number, reconciled openly when it slips.

## Texture & Tone

The register this universe plays best in: **deep-time melancholy over grimdark; ceremony over exposition; wonder that has paperwork.** Old things are treated with gravity. Institutions have liturgies. Danger is real but telegraphed, and quiet slice-of-life aboard a great hull is complete, valid play. The universe is vast and largely indifferent — warmth in it is *built*, by households and crews, which is exactly what makes it precious.

## Your Stratum (fill in — this is your campaign's sealed layer)

The bible above is the sky. Everything below is yours. Fill these slots and paste them with the bible; the framework's template-slot patterns apply.

- **YOUR HOUSE / POLITY:** name, holdings, reputation, one internal tension.
- **YOUR HULL:** class and scale (a titan-class estate? a courier with high hours?), its service history in three lines, its population if it has one *(a drift-prone quantity — pick a starting number)*.
- **YOUR PROTAGONIST:** who they are and — per the framework — their hard physical negatives, restricted-use tokens, and never-bench companions, if any.
- **YOUR DRIFT-PRONE QUANTITIES:** at minimum: treasury, elapsed time, and any population aboard.
- **YOUR AMBIGUITY FLAGS:** at minimum: what the Jump traverses, and one property of any maker object in your campaign.
- **YOUR HARD LINES:** content boundaries, stated as configuration, not vibes.

## Three Seeds (optional openings, universe-layer only)

**The Contested Salvage.** A hull of unknown origin has been pulled from the deep dark, and three parties hold plausible claims: the salvor crew, a registry house, and a broker whose client will not be named. The surveys of the hull disagree with each other. Your protagonist holds — or is offered — a stake.

**The Frame at Auction.** A Twilight frame has surfaced at a relic auction, provenance immaculate, price already indecent. The night before the sale, the frame does something frames at rest are not supposed to do. The auction house would very much like this handled quietly.

**The First Jump.** Slice-of-life opening: your protagonist's first Jump aboard a great hull — the preparation, the ceremony, the crossing felt in the body, and the neighborhood politics of the ship waiting on the far side. No crisis. The world is a place, not a plot; begin by living in it.
